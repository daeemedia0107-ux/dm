**Industry:** Food & Beverage / Subscription Service

## The Problem

When Protein Pals came to us, they had a good product and no system to sell it.

Three Indian co-founders running a high-protein meal delivery service across the Greater Toronto Area — Ajax, Brampton, Scarborough, Markham, Mississauga, and more. Subscription-based. Indian fusion recipes. Multiple plan options: starter packs, weekly, monthly, muscle gain, fat loss, veg and non-veg, breakfast-lunch-dinner combinations.

The offering was solid. The business wasn't.

They had 15–20 unreliable subscriptions at best. They'd spent 1–3k CAD on event sponsorships targeting Indian audiences and converted zero. Not one subscriber from those events.

Their Instagram had under 300 followers. No human face on the brand — just photos of tiffins and food with no context. No one watching could understand what the service was, how it worked, what plans were available, or how to subscribe. The offer wasn't clear. The product wasn't clear. The system didn't exist.

They were posting content, but it wasn't working because it was built without a strategy. No eating experience shown. No tasting reactions. No social proof. No clear path from "this looks interesting" to "I want to subscribe."

The core issue wasn't their food. It was that no system existed to turn attention into subscriptions.

---

## The Diagnosis

Before we spent a single rupee — or dollar — on ads, we identified three root causes:

**1. The brand had no face.** People buy from people, especially when it comes to food. Protein Pals was showing tiffins, not humans. No founders on camera. No customers tasting. No real-life experience of the service. In a trust-driven business like meal delivery, this is fatal.

**2. The offer wasn't visible.** They had multiple plans — starter, weekly, monthly, muscle gain, fat loss, veg, non-veg — but none of this was clear to someone landing on their page. A potential customer would have to dig through the website to understand what they'd actually get. The Instagram told them nothing.

**3. There was no acquisition system.** They were relying on word of mouth and event sponsorships. No ads. No automation. No CRM. No way to capture interest, qualify it, and convert it. Every potential subscriber who showed interest was being lost because there was no pipeline to catch them.

This wasn't a marketing problem. It was a system problem.

## The Strategy

We built the system in three phases: foundation, launch, and scale.

### Phase 1: Fix the Foundation (Before Ads)

We gave Protein Pals two pieces of feedback before onboarding them — and both started working immediately.

**Pinned post carousels on Instagram.** We asked them to create carousel posts for their highest-selling packages — each slide showing the dish, the macros, the price, and actual photos of the food. Not stock images. Real meals. We pinned these to the top of their Instagram so that anyone landing on the page would instantly see: what the plans are, what the food looks like, what the nutritional breakdown is, and how much it costs. The offer became visible in seconds.

**UGC creator outreach.** We told them to reach out to Indian creators in Canada. Not for sales. Not for reach. For content. We told them follower count doesn't matter. The goal was to get multiple real faces on the page — people tasting the food, showing the packaging, talking about the experience. This content would serve two purposes: warm up the Instagram page for anyone who visits, and give us creative assets to use in paid campaigns later. Sales would be a secondary benefit. Trust was the primary objective.

These two moves — before we spent anything on ads — gave Protein Pals their first taste of traction.

### Phase 2: Build the Machine (June–July 2025)

We directed their first ad shoot at the end of May 2025. Multiple content formats: founders on camera, lifestyle shots, packaging, real kitchen footage. By the time the shoot was done, edits reviewed, and the backend built, we launched our first campaigns by end of June.

**The backend system:**

We set up a complete acquisition pipeline:

- **Meta Lead Ads** as the front-end capture mechanism
- **ManyChat automation** as the qualification layer — when someone engaged with an ad, the automation triggered and asked them to tap "Menu" (to receive a PDF menu with dish photos) or "Trial" (for a free 2-day trial). From there, it captured their name, zip code, city, dietary preference (veg or non-veg), profession (student, WFO, WFH), and health goal
- **Zoho CRM** as the lead management system — every lead landed in the CRM with full qualification data, tagged and categorized
- **Conversion API** feeding CRM outcomes back to Meta — when a lead was marked as qualified, contacted, or converted in the CRM, that data looped back to Meta's ad optimization engine in real time

This feedback loop is where the magic happens. Meta's algorithm doesn't just optimize for lead volume — it optimizes for the kind of leads that actually convert. When you tell Meta "this lead became a paying customer" and "this lead was junk," the algorithm learns the difference. Over time, it finds more of the former and fewer of the latter.

**The creative strategy:**

We started with engagement ads paired with ManyChat to gauge the audience pool and validate the offer. Message conversations were coming in at 0.5 CAD each. The automation was doing its job — pooling audiences into categories, qualifying interest, and building custom audiences we could target later.

When we launched the first lead generation campaigns with the founders' ads, the leads that came in were already warmed up from the engagement phase. Quality was high from day one.

We tested multiple angles in those initial campaigns: founders talking directly to camera, a working women angle (busy professionals who need healthy meals), a muscle gain angle (fitness-focused audience), and casual street-style Q&A content. Each angle attracted a slightly different segment of the ICP — all valid, all converting.

**Result by mid-July:** 45 new subscriptions closed in less than 15 days.

### Phase 3: Scale With UGC (July–October 2025)

With the founders' ads performing at 3–4 CAD per lead, we moved to the next lever: UGC-led campaigns.

The creators Protein Pals had reached out to — based on our initial guidance — had been posting content on the page. Real people, real food, real reactions. With their consent, we took that UGC content and launched it as paid ad creative.

The results were immediate. UGC campaigns dropped the cost per lead from 3–4 CAD down to 2–3 CAD with close to zero junk. Real people talking about real food performed better than founder-led ads because it felt like a recommendation, not a pitch.

**Then Meta started rejecting our ads.**

Starting in August, ad after ad was flagged and rejected. The reasons Meta gave were generic and contradictory — "forms collecting data" was cited as a reason, even though Meta's own lead gen forms are designed to collect data. We spoke to Meta support. We challenged the reasoning. We got nowhere.

CPL spiked from 2–3 CAD to 4–5 CAD, then 8–9 CAD in September.

We aren't shy about sharing great results. We also aren't shy about sharing the hard parts. This was hard. When your ads get rejected and you don't know exactly why, and Meta won't tell you, the morale of the entire team takes a hit. You plan campaigns, build creative, set targeting — and then the platform decides to reject it without a clear explanation.

But we kept going. We iterated on creatives, overlays, form structures, automation triggers, and question formats. We tested 4 different versions of everything — 4 creatives, 4 forms, 4 campaigns — on minimal budget, with one goal: find what Meta wouldn't reject, then scale it.

## The Hidden Insight

60–70% of the leads that converted were vegetarian.

This wasn't something Protein Pals expected. But the data from our CRM — which captured dietary preference on every lead — made it undeniable. The majority of their paying subscriber base preferred veg meals.

This insight reshaped the entire strategy. We adjusted ad creatives to lead with vegetarian dishes. We briefed UGC creators to feature veg meals prominently. We restructured targeting to lean into this audience segment.

The point: a lead generation system isn't just about generating leads. It's about generating intelligence. The CRM data didn't just help us optimize ads — it helped Protein Pals understand their own market better than they did before.

## The System (What We Actually Built)

This case study isn't about ads. It's about a system. Here's what was running behind the scenes:

`[DIAGRAM: Meta (audience-facing) → Form Data → CRM → Real-time lead updates → Ads Manager → Activity Tracking → back to Meta]`

`[SCREENSHOT: LeadChain in Zoho CRM — showing field mapping: City, Country, Last Name, First Name, Email, Phone, Zip Code, State all syncing between lead forms and CRM]`

**The loop works like this:**

1. A prospect sees an ad on Instagram or Facebook
2. They engage — tapping "Menu" or "Trial" in the ManyChat automation
3. The automation qualifies them by capturing dietary preference, location, profession, and health goal
4. Their data flows into Zoho CRM, categorized and tagged
5. The founders call the lead, have the conversation, and update the CRM with the outcome — qualified, contacted, closed, junk
6. That outcome data flows back to Meta via the Conversion API
7. Meta's algorithm uses that feedback to find more leads like the ones who converted — and fewer like the ones who didn't

Every cycle makes the system smarter. Every lead gives it more data. Every conversion teaches it what a good customer looks like. That's why the CPL dropped from 3–4 CAD to 1.13 CAD over time — the system was learning.

This is the difference between running ads and building a revenue system.
